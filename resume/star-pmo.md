# STAR-AD Storytelling - Product Manager / PMO Angle

## Framework

Use this structure to prepare interview answers, resume bullets, and recruiter conversations:

- **S - Situation:** business context, customer problem, ambiguity, constraints.
- **T - Task:** your responsibility and ownership.
- **A - Action:** what you did, how you did it, and who you worked with.
- **R - Result:** measurable or observable impact.
- **A - Analysis:** what you learned, why the decision mattered, and how you reasoned.
- **D - Differentiator:** what makes your profile stronger than a generic PM, PMO, or data architect.

## Case 1: LatAm BI Data Warehouse / Order-to-Cash Analytics Product

### Situation

At 3M LatAm, business users initially asked for simple reports, but the real problem was deeper: information was fragmented across regions, KPI definitions were inconsistent, and different areas had competing interpretations of the same metrics. The process scope covered Order-to-Cash, including order management, fulfillment, shipping/logistics, invoicing, collections, and payment.

### Task

I was responsible for leading product discovery, aligning stakeholders across fragmented LatAm entities, translating business needs into technical requirements, and turning a reporting request into a scalable analytics product for 500+ executives and business users.

### Action

I led stakeholder interviews, workshops, shadowing, process analysis, prototyping, and formal Design Sprint sessions co-led with Business Process. I worked with lead users to identify the real decision-making pain points, define KPI ownership, and separate analytics needs by operational, tactical, and executive decision levels.

I converted business requirements into technical requirements, Jira-ready user stories, acceptance criteria, definition of done, dependency mapping, and sprint sequencing. Stakeholders defined business priority, while I prioritized technically based on dependencies, team capacity, risk, and business value.

### Result

The result was the LatAm BI Data Warehouse: an Order-to-Cash analytics product used by 500+ executives and business users. It delivered 95% reporting consistency across the region and enabled timely, calculated, and reliable information for different decision levels, from operational data refreshed every few hours to executive views aligned with month-end close.

### Analysis

The key insight was that users did not simply need more reports; they needed a trusted way to make decisions. The product required shared definitions, data ownership, process ownership, and a clear analytics narrative by decision level.

### Differentiator

My differentiator is that I can operate across product discovery, business process, data modeling, BI storytelling, backlog readiness, and post-go-live product ownership. I am not only defining what should be built; I understand how the data product should work technically and how users will consume it.

## Case 2: Dashboard Storytelling By Decision Level

### Situation

Business users needed scorecards for supply chain and Order-to-Cash decisions, but a single dashboard view overloaded some users while lacking detail for others. Executives, tactical leaders, and operational users needed different levels of detail, refresh frequency, and analysis flow.

### Task

I needed to design the analytics storytelling so each audience received the right indicators, level of detail, and refresh frequency for its decision-making context.

### Action

I divided scorecards into operational, tactical, and executive views. I defined which KPIs belonged to each level, how frequently each layer should refresh, how users should drill down from summary to detail, and how the analytical flow should support decisions instead of simply displaying data.

### Result

Users consumed information aligned to their roles. Operational users received fresher information to act quickly, while executive users received consolidated views for strategic follow-up and month-end decision-making.

### Analysis

Dashboard storytelling was not just a visual design activity. It started with the analytical model, KPI definitions, data quality, refresh cadence, and the user's decision journey.

### Differentiator

I design BI products from the decision experience backward, connecting business context, data model, KPI logic, drilldowns, and adoption.

## Case 3: Product Ownership After Go-Live

### Situation

After analytics products went live, business needs continued to evolve. Users requested enhancements, KPI changes, new views, support, governance improvements, and SLA adjustments.

### Task

I remained responsible for the product after implementation, not only during discovery or delivery.

### Action

I managed ongoing support, roadmap evolution, enhancement prioritization, governance, SLAs, data quality, and user adoption. I also aligned data owners and process owners so issues could be escalated and resolved with clear accountability.

### Result

The analytics product became a living platform rather than a one-time technical delivery. It maintained trust, supported continuous improvement, and gave the business a stable mechanism for evolving reporting and decision-making needs.

### Analysis

A data product loses value quickly if there is no ownership after go-live. Governance, quality, user adoption, and roadmap management are part of the product lifecycle.

### Differentiator

I bring end-to-end ownership: discovery, delivery, operations, governance, quality, adoption, and evolution.

## Case 4: Stakeholder Negotiation And Backlog Prioritization

### Situation

Finance, Sales, Operations, Supply Chain, Manufacturing, and other business areas often had different priorities, conflicting requirements, and inconsistent metric definitions.

### Task

I needed to align stakeholders, negotiate scope and trade-offs, and convert business priorities into an executable technical backlog.

### Action

I facilitated stakeholder conversations, documented requirements, reported team capacity, mapped dependencies, identified risks, supported resource allocation, and justified investment needs. When requirements conflicted or changed mid-development, I proposed alternatives and escalated decisions to the right stakeholders.

### Result

The team advanced with clearer priorities, fewer ambiguities, common KPI definitions, and a stronger single source of truth across the region.

### Analysis

Prioritization is not just ordering tasks. It requires making trade-offs visible: value, effort, dependencies, capacity, risk, timing, and stakeholder impact.

### Differentiator

I can translate business tension into executable delivery decisions for technical teams.
